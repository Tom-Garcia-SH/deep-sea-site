Sea Angels

The sea angel earns its name from its graceful movement, gliding through the water by rhythmically flapping its wing-like appendages. Despite its delicate appearance, it is a deep-sea predator, using its buccal cones to extract prey directly from their shells. Highly selective in its diet, the sea angel feeds exclusively on sea butterflies.

Sea angels are found worldwide, inhabiting depths ranging from the ocean's surface to 2,000 meters. They also play a crucial role in the marine food chain, serving as an important food source for various animals, including jellyfish, birds, and baleen whales.

Ocean acidification and rising temperatures pose a significant threat to sea angel populations, primarily due to their impact on the sea butterfly, their main food source. The sea butterfly's shell is made of calcium carbonate, and the key components required to form it are becoming increasingly scarce. This depletion could lead to a decline in sea butterfly populations, ultimately affecting the sea angels that depend on them.
